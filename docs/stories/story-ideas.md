# Story Ideas: Truth-Seekers and Myth-Breakers

A collection of ten graphic novel story ideas featuring people whose courage, curiosity, and careful thinking reshaped how we detect misinformation and understand knowledge itself. Each story is designed to inspire young readers to ask Sofia's favorite question: *But how do we know?*

## 1. Rachel Carson and the Silent Spring

In 1962, a quiet marine biologist took on the most powerful chemical companies in the world — armed only with evidence. Rachel Carson noticed something strange: songbirds were disappearing from American suburbs. Following the trail, she uncovered how DDT, marketed as a miracle pesticide, was silently destroying entire ecosystems. Chemical companies called her hysterical, unscientific, and worse. But Carson had done her homework: every claim in *Silent Spring* was meticulously footnoted. The story follows her race against terminal cancer to finish the book, her televised confrontations with industry scientists, and the moment President Kennedy ordered an investigation that vindicated her. Young readers learn how one person with rigorous evidence can overcome a well-funded disinformation campaign — and why peer-reviewed science matters.

**Key TOK themes:** Evidence-based reasoning, conflicts of interest in science, the distinction between knowledge and marketing.

## 2. Ignaz Semmelweis and the Invisible Killer

Vienna, 1847. In one maternity ward, mothers were dying at horrifying rates. In another, just down the hall, they were fine. Dr. Ignaz Semmelweis became obsessed with the mystery. Through careful observation and statistical analysis — decades before germ theory existed — he discovered the cause: doctors were going straight from dissecting corpses to delivering babies without washing their hands. When Semmelweis ordered handwashing with chlorinated lime, deaths plummeted. But the medical establishment rejected his findings because they couldn't explain *why* it worked, and because the implication — that doctors themselves were killing patients — was too painful to accept. Semmelweis died in an asylum, ridiculed. Twenty years later, Pasteur proved him right. The story explores how correct knowledge can be rejected when it threatens identity and authority.

**Key TOK themes:** The Semmelweis reflex, confirmation bias, the social construction of scientific consensus.

## 3. Ida B. Wells and the Data Journalist

In 1892, after three of her friends were lynched in Memphis, Ida B. Wells did something radical: she counted. Armed with a notebook and a train ticket, she traveled the American South documenting every lynching she could verify — names, dates, circumstances, newspaper accounts. Her pamphlet *Southern Horrors* used the lynchers' own words to dismantle the myth that lynchings were about protecting white women. Her data showed most victims were targeted for economic or political reasons. Threatened with death, her newspaper office burned, Wells carried her evidence to England and embarrassed America on the world stage. This story shows young readers how raw data, carefully gathered, can shatter a carefully constructed lie — and introduces the foundations of modern data journalism.

**Key TOK themes:** Quantitative evidence, primary sources, how dominant narratives hide truth.

## 4. Alice Stewart and the X-Ray Mystery

In the 1950s, British epidemiologist Alice Stewart noticed that childhood leukemia rates were rising. Through a painstaking survey of thousands of families, she discovered a shocking correlation: mothers who had received a single prenatal X-ray were twice as likely to have children who developed cancer. The medical establishment — invested in X-ray technology — attacked her methods for twenty-five years. But Stewart kept refining her statistics with the help of mathematician George Kneale, who became her partner in stress-testing every objection raised against her. Eventually, the evidence became overwhelming. The story dramatizes the long, patient work of defending a truth that powerful institutions don't want to hear, and shows how statistical thinking protects knowledge from wishful thinking.

**Key TOK themes:** Epidemiology, replication, the difference between being wrong and being unpopular.

## 5. Carl Sagan and the Baloney Detection Kit

Before his death in 1996, Carl Sagan gave the world a gift: a set of tools for thinking clearly in an age of nonsense. In *The Demon-Haunted World*, he laid out what he called the "baloney detection kit" — a checklist for evaluating claims about UFOs, faith healers, and fake science. This story follows young Carl growing up in Brooklyn, awestruck by a library card and the World's Fair, through his work on the Voyager missions, to his final battle with illness as he raced to finish a book warning humanity about the coming darkness of scientific illiteracy. Sagan's genius was making critical thinking feel like wonder, not cynicism. Young readers see that skepticism is not the opposite of awe — it's what keeps awe honest.

**Key TOK themes:** Scientific literacy, skepticism vs. cynicism, the ethics of public knowledge.

## 6. Marie Colvin and the Eyewitness

Some lies can only be broken by someone who was there. War correspondent Marie Colvin lost an eye in Sri Lanka reporting on a civil war the government claimed wasn't happening. She kept going. In 2012, she slipped into the besieged city of Homs, Syria, to report on civilians the Assad regime insisted were "terrorists." Her final broadcast, made hours before she was killed in a targeted shelling, described a baby dying in front of her. Colvin understood that propaganda thrives on distance — that regimes lie most confidently when no witnesses remain. This story (handled with appropriate gravity for young readers) asks what we owe to truth and who bears the cost of bringing it home.

**Key TOK themes:** Eyewitness testimony, propaganda, the ethics of journalism.

## 7. Hedy Lamarr and the Hidden Inventor

Hollywood in the 1940s saw only Hedy Lamarr's face. What it missed was her mind. Born in Austria, she had fled a Nazi arms-dealer husband, memorizing technical conversations at his dinner parties. During World War II, horrified by news of torpedoed civilian ships, she co-invented a frequency-hopping signal system that would make Allied torpedoes unjammable. The Navy ignored her — she was a woman, an actress, and a foreigner. Her patent expired unused. Decades later, her invention became the foundation of Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, and GPS. The story exposes how society can ignore real knowledge because of who holds it, and how dangerous it is to judge ideas by the reputation of the person who offers them.

**Key TOK themes:** The genetic fallacy, bias in who gets to be called a "knower," hidden histories.

## 8. Maurice Hilleman and the Vaccine Detective

You have probably never heard of Maurice Hilleman, but he may have saved your life. This gruff, foul-mouthed microbiologist developed more than forty vaccines, including eight of the fourteen given routinely to children today. When his own daughter Jeryl Lynn came down with the mumps in 1963, he swabbed her throat in the middle of the night, drove the sample to his lab, and used it to develop the mumps vaccine still in use today. But Hilleman's real battle was against misinformation: when a 1998 paper falsely linked vaccines to autism and panic spread, Hilleman was one of the first scientists to trace the fraud. The story follows his life's work and introduces young readers to how scientific consensus is built — and why a single retracted paper can still fuel misinformation decades later.

**Key TOK themes:** Scientific consensus, replication crisis, how fraud enters the knowledge system.

## 9. Hannah Arendt and the Truth in the Courtroom

In 1961, philosopher Hannah Arendt traveled to Jerusalem to cover the trial of Adolf Eichmann, one of the architects of the Holocaust. She expected to see a monster. Instead, she saw a mediocre bureaucrat who claimed he was "just following orders." Her reporting coined a phrase that shook the world: *the banality of evil*. Arendt's insight — that terrible things are often done by ordinary people who stop thinking critically — made her enemies everywhere. Friends accused her of excusing Eichmann; others said she was too harsh on Jewish leaders. But Arendt refused to simplify. This story shows young readers that thinking for yourself — really thinking, against pressure from every direction — is the hardest and most important thing a human being can do.

**Key TOK themes:** Moral reasoning, the refusal to think as a political act, the courage to be misunderstood.

## 10. Katalin Karikó and the Rejected Idea

For forty years, Hungarian biochemist Katalin Karikó believed that messenger RNA could be used to make medicines and vaccines. For forty years, almost no one else did. She was demoted at the University of Pennsylvania, denied grants over and over, and nearly deported. She kept working. Partnering with immunologist Drew Weissman, she solved the central problem that had made mRNA useless in the body. When COVID-19 emerged in 2020, her rejected idea became the foundation of vaccines that saved millions of lives within months. In 2023 she won the Nobel Prize. This story is a love letter to stubborn curiosity — and a warning about how scientific gatekeeping can bury the truth for decades. Young readers learn that being right early is often indistinguishable from being wrong, and that the scientific community is not infallible.

**Key TOK themes:** Scientific revolutions, the sociology of knowledge, persistence against consensus.

## 11. Galileo Galilei and the Moons of Jupiter

In 1610, Galileo pointed a homemade telescope at the night sky and saw something no human had ever seen: four tiny points of light orbiting Jupiter. If moons could circle Jupiter, then not everything revolved around the Earth — and the Church's official cosmology was wrong. Galileo did not want a fight with Rome. He was a devout Catholic who believed the "book of nature" and the "book of scripture" could both be true. But when he published his findings in Italian so ordinary people could read them, the Inquisition put him on trial. Forced to recant under threat of torture, he spent his last years under house arrest, going blind, still writing. Legend says he muttered *"And yet it moves"* as he left the courtroom. The story shows young readers how evidence from a simple instrument can overturn centuries of authority — and the cost of insisting on what you can see with your own eyes.

**Key TOK themes:** Observation vs. authority, the limits of institutional knowledge, science and religion as different ways of knowing.

## 12. Andreas Vesalius and the Forbidden Bodies

For over a thousand years, European medicine was based on the writings of the Roman physician Galen — who had never actually dissected a human body, because it was forbidden in his time. He dissected apes and pigs instead, and guessed the rest. Generation after generation of doctors memorized his errors as sacred truth. In 1543, a young Belgian anatomist named Andreas Vesalius decided to look for himself. He stole corpses from gallows at night, dissected them by candlelight, and published *On the Fabric of the Human Body* — a book with stunningly accurate illustrations showing Galen had been wrong about hundreds of things. The medical establishment was furious. How dare a young man contradict a thousand years of tradition? But Vesalius had the bodies, the drawings, and the truth. This story shows young readers how knowledge can calcify into dogma when nobody bothers to check the evidence — and how one careful observer can restart a whole field.

**Key TOK themes:** Appeal to authority, first-hand evidence, the courage to look.

## 13. Charles Darwin and the Finches' Beaks

Charles Darwin set sail on the HMS Beagle at twenty-two, a young man who had once considered becoming a country clergyman. Five years later, he came home with notebooks full of observations that troubled him deeply. The finches of the Galápagos Islands had different beaks on different islands, perfectly suited to each island's food. Fossils of extinct giant mammals looked suspiciously like smaller living ones. The evidence pointed somewhere Darwin knew would horrify Victorian society: species change over time, and humans are part of the same tree of life as every other animal. He sat on his theory for twenty years, afraid of the controversy, afraid of what it would do to his deeply religious wife Emma. Only when another naturalist independently reached the same conclusion did Darwin finally publish *On the Origin of Species*. The story explores how scientific honesty can be personally costly, and how the weight of accumulated evidence can force even a cautious mind to follow it wherever it leads.

**Key TOK themes:** Inductive reasoning, accumulation of evidence, the emotional dimension of paradigm shifts.

## 14. Mary Anning and the Dragons in the Cliffs

In the early 1800s, most English people believed the Earth was about 6,000 years old, and that every creature God had made was still alive somewhere. Then a poor girl from the seaside town of Lyme Regis began pulling monsters out of the cliffs. Mary Anning, who had never been to school, taught herself anatomy and geology so she could identify the strange fossils she found — ichthyosaurs, plesiosaurs, pterosaurs. Her discoveries were undeniable evidence that whole categories of animals had once existed and then vanished, which meant the world was vastly older than scripture suggested and that extinction was real. Male scientists bought her fossils, published papers about them, and rarely credited her. Yet without Mary Anning's finds, geology and paleontology might have taken decades longer to break free from biblical timelines. The story honors an outsider whose sharp eye and stubborn labor helped rewrite humanity's understanding of deep time.

**Key TOK themes:** Deep time, extinction, who gets credited as a "knower."
